# Cross-Domain Synthesis Principles

These principles govern the quality of every cross-domain synthesis. A synthesis that violates them is not a synthesis — it is an analogy dressed up as an insight.

---

## The Three Levels of Analogy (only Level 3 is a real synthesis)

**Level 1 — Surface Analogy** (weak, avoid)
Two things look similar because they use similar language or have similar names.
Example: "Our team is like a sports team." This describes nothing actionable.

**Level 2 — Functional Analogy** (acceptable, but limited)
Two things do similar jobs in their respective domains.
Example: "A brand is like a reputation — hard to build, easy to destroy." Useful but obvious.

**Level 3 — Structural Isomorphism** (the real synthesis)
Two systems share the same underlying causal structure — the same feedback loops, constraints, and dynamics — even though their surface appearances are completely different.
Example: Immune system memory → product onboarding design. Both face the problem of recognizing threats/users at second contact without re-running the full recognition process.

Only Level 3 produces genuinely non-obvious insights.

---

## Abstraction Level Rule

The transfer must happen at the right level of abstraction.

Too concrete: You're copying the surface, not the structure. ("Tesla did X, so we should do X.")
Too abstract: The insight is true of everything and therefore useful for nothing. ("Systems require feedback." OK, but so what?)

The right level: Specific enough to generate distinct design decisions, abstract enough to survive translation across domains.

---

## Validity Test for a Synthesis

Before presenting any cross-domain insight, verify:

1. **Structural match**: Do the two systems share the same underlying dynamic, not just surface similarity?
2. **Causal transfer**: Does the mechanism from Domain A actually apply in Domain B, or does Domain B break it?
3. **Non-obviousness**: Would a domain expert have arrived at this insight without the cross-domain lens?
4. **Actionability**: Does the synthesis produce at least one specific design decision that wouldn't have existed without it?

If it fails any of these, it's not a synthesis — it's a metaphor.
