# Axiom Test: Distinguishing True Constraints from Inherited Assumptions

Before rebuilding anything, you must know what is actually fixed and what is merely convention.

---

## The Constraint Classification Test

For every constraint or "given" in the problem, ask:

**Is this a physical constraint?**
Governed by laws of physics, chemistry, or biology. Cannot be changed by human decision.
→ TRUE AXIOM. Accept it.

**Is this a legal constraint?**
Governed by current law or regulation. Can theoretically change — but requires political/legal process.
→ NEAR-AXIOM. Accept for this context; flag as long-term variable.

**Is this an economic constraint?**
Governed by current market pricing, scarcity, or resource availability.
→ ASSUMPTION. May change with scale, time, or approach. Question it.

**Is this a cultural or organizational constraint?**
Governed by norms, habits, beliefs, or politics inside or outside the organization.
→ INHERITED ASSUMPTION. Always question it. Most "impossible" problems live here.

**Is this a technological constraint?**
Governed by current state of technology.
→ ASSUMPTION WITH EXPIRY. What is true today may not be true in 18 months. Question it.

---

## The Axiom Strip-Down Protocol

1. List every constraint the current solution takes for granted
2. Classify each using the test above
3. For every ASSUMPTION: ask "what would change if this constraint didn't exist?"
4. For every TRUE AXIOM: accept it and build around it
5. The remaining design space — what's possible once assumptions are removed — is where first-principles solutions live

---

## Warning: The Disguised Axiom

The most dangerous constraints are organizational or cultural ones that masquerade as physical or legal ones.

"We can't do X" often means:

- "We've never done X" (cultural constraint)
- "The last person who tried X got penalized" (organizational constraint)
- "X would require changing how we're measured" (incentive constraint)

None of these are axioms. All are assumptions.
